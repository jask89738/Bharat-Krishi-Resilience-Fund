Author's note
Blended finance in India is often discussed in pitch decks but rarely measured in the open. As an MBA student of Sustainable Finance, I built this dashboard to test whether the UNDP 6-pillar framework — policy, capital, pipeline, risk, impact, transparency — can be operationalized into something a state government, a DFI, or an LP can read in under five minutes.

The sector chosen is climate-resilient agriculture (micro-irrigation), where India has both a deep concessional pool and a maturing private appetite. All figures are hypothetical but benchmarked against the Convergence Blended Finance Database, CPI India's 2024 climate finance flows, and SAMRIDH/Vertelo case studies.

The intent is not a finished product. It is a credible prototype — small enough to iterate on, structured enough to defend in a board room.

Key insight (300 words)
Capital mobilization is the strongest pillar — a 4.5x blended multiplier and 23 unique private investors signal that concessional first-loss cover (12%) is effectively de-risking commercial entry. Impact delivery is also on-track, with 142,500 tonnes of CO2e avoided and 48,700 households reached through 31,200 micro-irrigation installations.

Transparency is the weakest pillar (score 55/100). While reporting is quarterly and third-party verified, deal-level terms are only partially public, limiting peer benchmarking and crowding-in of new LPs. Pipeline quality (62/100) is the second concern: disbursement reached only 68% of committed capital, indicating a thinner-than-expected backlog of bankable FPO and agri-MSME projects.

Urgent intervention should target two actions: (1) publish standardized term sheets and concessionality ratios on a public portal, and (2) fund a dedicated origination cell with state agri-departments and NABARD to convert the 32% undeployed capital into pipeline within two quarters.
